[1a] Chromatin, the complex of DNA and proteins organizing the Eukaryotic genome, plays a pivotal role in orchestrating a multitude of genomic functions, including DNA replication and the precise regulation of gene activity.  
[1b] Its structure exhibits a hierarchical organization, with biological function affected by structural changes across all levels of the hierarchy.  
[1b] The fundamental unit of chromatin is the nucleosome, an assembly comprising of approx. 150 base pairs (bp) of DNA wrapped around a histone octamer.  
[1b] Nucleosomes are not simply barriers preventing access to genomic DNA; rather, they can be better understood as flexible platforms on which key biological processes can be organized.  
[1b] For example, nucleosomal DNA sliding and unwrapping from histones modulate transcription factor (TF) binding, with downstream effects on gene expression.  
[1c] At a higher hierarchical level, these nucleosomes interact together to form chromatin fibers exhibiting remarkable structural polymorphism.
