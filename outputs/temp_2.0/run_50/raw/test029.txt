[1a] A rise in circulating catecholamine (CA) levels is a crucial step triggered by the organism to cope with a stressful situation.
[1a] By releasing both epinephrine (E) and norepinephrine (NE), the adrenal medullary tissue crucially contributes to this response.
[1c] Beyond the beneficial effect of CA secretion elicited by an acute stress, sustained and/or repetitive CA secretion episodes (in response to chronic stressful situations for example) can have deleterious outcomes [1], as unveiled by the elevated blood pressure observed in response to chronic infusion of E in rat [2,3] or in chronically cold stressed rats [4].
[1c] It has long been reported that increased blood pressure is associated with a sympathetic nervous system hyperactivity leading to a raised neural tone [5,6] and increased plasma E and NE [7].
[1c] The adrenal medulla being the unique source of circulating E, this assigns the secretory function of the medulla and more generally the sympatho-adrenal axis as critical determinants of arterial hypertension pathogenesis [8–10].
[2b] Reciprocally, and despite it is indisputable that the adrenal medulla primarily contributes to enhance blood pressure, whether and how a ‘hypertensive’ environment impacts the adrenal secretory function itself remains elusive.
[3a] We address this issue in the adult spontaneously hypertensive rat, in which the circulating E levels gradually increase as arterial hypertension develops and then stabilize in adult animals to become comparable to those in normotensive rats [11].
[2b] data suggests that the adrenal gland develops shielding mechanisms dedicated to normalize blood CA amounts, and to date nothing is known on the adaptive mechanisms elaborated by the neuroendocrine chromaffin cells to adjust the release of CA.
[1c] Adrenal CA secretion and more generally the adrenomedullary tissue function are controlled by the coordination of interconnected and complex pathways [12].
[1b] The initial incoming command comes from the sympathetic nervous system that releases mainly acetylcholine (ACh) but also neuropeptides (PACAP, VIP…) at splanchnic nerve terminals synapsing onto chromaffin cells [13,14].
