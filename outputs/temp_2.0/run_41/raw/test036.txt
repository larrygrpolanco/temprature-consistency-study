[1a] Muscles actively change length to power everyday movement.  
[1a] But despite being the motors of movement, muscles function surprisingly better as brakes than motors (1).  
[1a] Accordingly, muscle forces are enhanced during and following active muscle stretch (i.e., eccentric contraction) compared with contractions at a constant length (i.e., isometric contraction) or during active muscle shortening (i.e., concentric contraction) when muscle activity is matched (2â€“5).  
[1a] Accordingly, producing a given force eccentrically requires less muscle activity compared with an isometric or a concentric contraction.  
[1a] Since active muscle volume theoretically determines energy expenditure (6), the neuromuscular efficiency of eccentric contractions is therefore increased (7, 8).
