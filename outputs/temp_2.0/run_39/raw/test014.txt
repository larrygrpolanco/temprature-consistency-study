[1a] A hallmark of cancer cells that distinguishes them from normal differentiated cells is their unrestricted proliferative ability.
[1b] Numerous genetic, signaling, and metabolic pathways have been implicated in cancer proliferation.
[1c] Broadly, these factors are divided into pro-proliferative and anti-proliferative ones [1].
[1c] The former includes those that promote DNA and protein synthesis including PI3 kinase and Ras-MAPK signaling, chromosome segregation, and telomere extension just to name a few, whereas the latter acts as a brake for proliferation including the tumor suppressors PTEN and p53 [2, 3].
[1b] Genetic mutation of both positive and negative regulators has been causally linked to various cancer types.
[1a] Not surprisingly, hampering cancer proliferation is a primary target of cancer drugs, for example, paclitaxel (Taxol), the most prescribed drugs for treating cancers, acts by inhibiting microtubule assembly to prevent cell division [4].
[2b] Despite extensive studies, however, how cancer cells could escape from cell division regulation is not fully understood.
[1b] Cancer cells grow in a microenvironment wherein they closely interact with the extracellular matrix (ECM).
