[1b] Isoprene pyrophosphates are metabolites in the mevalonate pathway which is an essential metabolic pathway found in eukaryotes, archaea, and some prokaryotes [1] and is responsible for the multistep conversion of acetyl-CoA into isopentenyl pyrophosphate (IPPP) and its isomer dimethylallyl pyrophosphate (DMAPP).
[1b] IPPP and DMAPP are used as building blocks in the biosynthesis of all mammalian isoprenoids.
[1b] In addition, IPPP and DMAPP are used to produce a diverse array of essential nonsterol and sterol biomolecules.
[1a] An example is cholesterol which is crucial for maintaining cell membrane integrity and used for production of steroid hormones and bile acids.
[1a] Other products derived from isoprene PPs are lipoproteins, vitamin K, heme A, the mitochondrial electron carrier ubiquinone, dolichol, and isopentenyladenine used in tRNA modification [1, 2].
[1a] Consequently, the isoprene PPs produced by the mevalonate pathway are crucial for a plethora of important biological processes.
[1a] Figure 1 shows the structures of the physiologically relevant isoprene pyrophosphates (isoprene PPs) in animals.
[1b] Moreover, approximately 2% of all mammalian proteins undergo post-translational isoprenylation [3].
[1b] This process involves the covalent attachment of hydrophobic C-15 isoprene farnesyl or the C-20 isoprene geranyggeranyl group to cysteine residues in the C-terminal end of various proteins.
[1c] Proteins that are farnesylated include the small GTP-binding proteins (GTPases) exemplified by the Ras superfamily, whereas other small GTPases such as Rab and Rho GTPases are geranylgeranylated [4].
[1b] Prenylation of these proteins enables their attachment to cellular membranes, a prerequisite for these proteins to exert their specific functions in biological processes such as cell survival, cell signaling, and proliferation [5].
[1a] The dysregulation of isoprenoid biosynthesis has been implicated in several biochemical disorders, underscoring the crucial role of appropriate isoprenoid levels in maintaining proper cellular function [6–8).
