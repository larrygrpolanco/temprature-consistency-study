[1a] Muscles actively change length to power everyday movement.  
[1a] But despite being the motors of movement, muscles function surprisingly better as brakes than motors (1).  
[1b] Accordingly, muscle forces are enhanced during and following active muscle stretch (i.e., eccentric contraction) compared with contractions at a constant length (i.e., isometric contraction) or during active muscle shortening (i.e., concentric contraction) when muscle activity is matched (2–5).  
[1b] Accordingly, producing a given force eccentrically requires less muscle activity compared with an isometric or a concentric contraction.  
[1b] Since active muscle volume theoretically determines energy expenditure (6), the neuromuscular efficiency of eccentric contractions is therefore increased (7, 8).  
[1a] As eccentric contractions require less energy and muscle activity for equivalent force production, eccentric exercise has received considerable attention over the last decades to rehabilitate injuries, to improve athletic performance, and to help manage neuromuscular diseases (9–13).  
[1a] Accordingly, many training studies aimed to determine the specific adaptations of the muscle-tendon unit (MTU) to eccentric exercise in comparison to concentric exercise.  
[1c] The specific adaptations to eccentric exercise primarily include an increase in fascicle length (14).  
[2c] However, the fascicle length increases following eccentric exercise are highly variable among studies.  
[2a] By examining 15 in vivo human studies on training adaptations to eccentric exercise (11, 15–28), we found that fascicle length changed from −3% (i.e., decreased) to 34% (i.e., increased, see Figure S1).  
[2c] Accordingly, it is important to question why the fascicle length changes to eccentric exercise are so variable.  
[2b] One factor that could affect the muscle lengths and forces attained during and following eccentric contractions and the subsequent MTU adaptations following eccentric exercise is the pre-activation timing and level before MTU stretch.  
[1b] Because of the typical arrangement of a distal elastic tendon in series with a muscle within a lower limb MTU, the length changes of muscle and tendon can be decoupled (29, 30).  
[1b] For example, a muscle can still shorten against the elastic tendon while the MTU is stretched when there is no pre-activation.  
[2b] Accordingly, apparent eccentric exercises (i.e., MTU lengthening exercises) do not necessarily result in isolated eccentric contractions, but can result in a combination of muscle contraction types.  
[2b] Therefore, the highly variable fascicle length changes to apparent eccentric exercise could be due to different muscle fascicle behaviors during these exercises.  
[2b] During apparent eccentric exercise, the decoupling of muscle and tendon length changes can also affect the fascicle stretch amplitudes (31, 32).  
[2b] Differences in fascicle stretch amplitude during eccentric exercises might affect peak fascicle force during stretch, the residual force enhancement (rFE) following stretch (33–35), and the active and passive contributions to eccentric force production.  
[1c] This is because rFE was found to be related to the peak force and/or torque during stretch (33, 36, 37).  
[2c] However, it is unclear whether peak force is affected more by stretch amplitude or fascicle length (38).
