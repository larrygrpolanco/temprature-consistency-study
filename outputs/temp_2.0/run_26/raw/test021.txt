[1a] Biomolecular condensates serve as important mechanisms for the hierarchical and vectorial organization of chemistry within cells.
[1b] They are non-stoichiometric assemblies of biomolecules that can form via spontaneous or driven processes and exhibit characteristics of phase separation and percolation.
[1b] The chemical environment and physicochemical conditions within condensates are distinct from their surroundings, enabling cells to selectively partition biomolecules, prevent unwanted cross-talk and interference between various biochemical pathways, and buffer against chemical noise.
[1a] In recent years, these condensates have gained significant prominence and attracted substantial research interest.
[1b] They have been found to primarily form through the involvement of intrinsically disordered proteins (IDPs).
[1b] Multivalent interactions involve the binding of multiple partners simultaneously and are often nonspecific in nature, leading to the spontaneous aggregation and condensation of various cellular components.
[1b] These unique proteins lack a fixed three-dimensional structure, enabling them	to be highly flexible and engage in multivalent interactions.
[1c] Many research groups have attempted to establish what is known as the “molecular grammar”, which connects amino acid sequences with protein phase behaviors and the collective physical properties of condensates.
