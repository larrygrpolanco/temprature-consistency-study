[1a] The therapeutic use of messenger RNA (mRNA) has sparked great optimism in the development of novel vaccines and therapeutics against a myriad of infectious or yet incurable diseases (1).  
[1b] The mRNA technology enables the production of antigenic, functional, and/or therapeutic proteins by introducing mRNA into the human body and cells (2).  
[1b] Since mRNAs act in the cytoplasm transiently, they do not bear any risk of integration into the host cell genome.  
[1b] Most importantly, the mRNA technology enables rapid, cost-efficient and scalable production, which is free of cellular (cell cultures) or animal materials (3).  
[1a] Thus, mRNA technologies facilitate manufacturing and allow for a rapid response to emerging infectious diseases, as emphatically underscored by the rapid rollout of COVID-19 mRNA vaccines in many parts of the world.  
[1b] Modified nucleosides, such as pseudouridine (Œ®), N1-methylpseudouridine (m1Œ®) and 5-methylcytidine (m5C), are often incorporated into the mRNA molecules.  
[1c] Such modifications reduce stimulation of cytoplasmic RNA sensors, such as toll-like receptor 3 and 7, for improved safety profiles and enhanced mRNA translation (4, 5).  
[2b] However, how modified nucleosides affect mRNA concentration measurements and potentially confound pre-clinical dosing, efficacy, and toxicology studies, which could make or break further clinical development of any therapeutic, remains undefined.  
[1b] The determination of RNA concentration often relies on measurements of its UV absorbance at 260 nm wavelength (A260) and the implementation of the Beer‚ÄìLambert law (6).  
[1b] The accuracy of these measurements is scattered by the variable hypochromicity of RNA due to its sequence-dependent folding.

[1b] This difference is buffer- and concentration-dependent and arises from changes in the chemical environment of the nucleobases ‚Äì the main chromophore, due to base-pairing, stacking, intermolecular interactions, and other conformational changes.

[2b] Yet it would be valuable to know concentrations at higher accuracy for the development of mRNA technologies.  
[2b] Our particular concern is in measurements of self-amplifying RNAs (saRNA) and nucleoside-modified mRNAs.  
[1b] The logarithmic amplification of saRNA can convert a 20% accepted error in RNA concentration into a several fold differences in dose-response between one experiment and the subsequent replicates.  
[2b] The chemical modifications on the nucleobases of mRNA can also induce profound changes in the mRNA MAC, hindering the accurate quantification of nucleoside-modified mRNA concentrations.  
[1b] To attain greater accuracy in RNA quantification, RNA molecules are hydrolysed prior to UV absorbance determination using a combination of thermal and alkaline hydrolysis (6, 8).  
[1b] The RNA hydrolysis shifts the hypochromic folded state of the RNA to the hyperchromic state of the single monophosphate nucleotides (9).  
[1c] Since the precise MAC of the four standard nucleotides in aqueous buffered solution are known, the molar absorption of any hydrolysed mRNA can be calculated as the sum of the molar absorption of its nucleotide compositions.
